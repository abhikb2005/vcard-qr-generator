What Is V1CE?

V1CE (pronounced “vice”) is a UK-based digital business card platform that combines physical NFC smart cards with hosted digital profiles. When someone taps your V1CE card against their phone, it opens a web-based profile page with your name, title, photo, social links, and a “Save Contact” button. The platform also supports QR code sharing, Apple/Google Wallet passes, email signatures, and CRM integrations with HubSpot, Salesforce, and Pipedrive.

V1CE sells physical NFC cards in materials ranging from PVC (£59.99) to bamboo (£82.50), metal (£110), and 24K gold (£330). The digital profile is free for one card, but analytics, CRM sync, automated follow-ups, and team management require the Plus subscription at $4.99/month. For users who just need a vCard QR code for their existing business cards, V1CE’s hardware-first approach is expensive overkill.

Why Look for a V1CE Alternative?

  • Requires buying a physical card — V1CE’s core product is a physical NFC card starting at £59.99. If you already have printed business cards and just need a QR code to add to them, buying a separate NFC card is an unnecessary expense.
  • Subscription for full features — The free plan gives you one digital card with only 30 days of analytics. CRM integrations, automated follow-ups, unlimited analytics, and team management all require the Plus plan at $4.99/month ($60/year). The Teams plan jumps to $149.99/month.
  • Hosted profile pages, not standard vCards — V1CE doesn’t create standard vCard files. It creates hosted profile pages on V1CE’s servers. When someone taps or scans, they visit a web page and must click “Save Contact” manually. This adds friction compared to a direct vCard QR code that saves contact info immediately on scan.
  • Server dependency — Every V1CE interaction goes through their servers. If V1CE’s service goes down, or if they change their business model, your physical NFC cards become expensive pieces of plastic or metal.
  • Privacy trade-offs — V1CE tracks scan analytics, lead capture data, and CRM syncs. While they claim GDPR alignment, every contact interaction is processed through their servers. For privacy-first contact sharing, this may not be acceptable.
  • NFC compatibility issues — NFC requires the recipient’s phone to support NFC and have it enabled. Older phones and some Android devices may not read NFC reliably. QR codes work on every smartphone with a camera.
